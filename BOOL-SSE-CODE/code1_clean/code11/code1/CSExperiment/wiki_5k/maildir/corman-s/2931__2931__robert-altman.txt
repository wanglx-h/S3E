| birth_place = Kansas City, Missouri, United States | death_date = | death_place = Los Angeles, California, United States | occupation = | years_active = 1947–2006 | spouse = LaVonne Elmer (1946–1951)Lotus Corelli (1954–1957)Kathryn Reed (1959–2006) }} '''Robert Bernard Altman''' (February 20, 1925 – November 20, 2006) was an Americans movie director. He was born in Kansas City, Missouri and died of leukemia in Los Angeles, California. In 2006, the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences recognized his body of work with an Academy Honorary Award. His movies ''M*A*S*H (film)'' (1970), ''McCabe and Mrs. Miller'' (1971), and ''Nashville (film)'' (1975) have been selected to keep in the United States National Film Registry. ==Personal life== In the 1960s, Altman lived for nine years with his second wife in Mandeville Canyon in Brentwood, Los Angeles, California.Peter Biskind, ''Easy Riders, Raging Bulls'', New York: Touchstone Books, 1998 He moved to Malibu, California but in 1981 sold that home and the Lion's Gate production company. "I had no choice", he told the ''New York Times''. "Nobody was answering the phone" after the flop of ''Popeye''. He moved his family and business headquarters to New York. Eventually he moved back to Malibu, where he lived until his death. In November 2000, he said he would move to Paris if George W. Bush were elected. He joked that he had meant Paris, Texas when Bush became president. Altman was an outspoken cannabis (drug) user. He was a member of the NORML advisory board. He was against the 2003 invasion of Iraq."20 Questions, 2 Choices", ''The Birmingham News'', June 3, 2005 ==Death== Altman died on November 20, 2006, at age 81 at Cedars-Sinai Medical Center in Los Angeles. He died of complications from leukemia. Altman is survived by his wife, Kathryn Reed Altman; six children, Christine Westphal, Mike Altman, Stephen Altman (his production designer of choice for many movies), Connie Corriere, Robert Reed Altman, and Matthew Altman; 12 grandchildren; and five great-grandchildren.
