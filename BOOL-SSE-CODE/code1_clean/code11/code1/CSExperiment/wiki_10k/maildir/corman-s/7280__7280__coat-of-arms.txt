Subject: Coat of arms
A '''coat of arms''' is a wikt:unique wikt:design Painting on a shield. These designs may be inheritance, meaning that they pass from a father to his children. In the Middle Ages, these designs were shown on real shields, but today they are usually only drawing or painted on the paper that makes them wikt:legal which is called a '''grant of arms'''. The grant of arms is usually allowed only when it is given by the government of a country or its wikt:agent. In England, this is called the College of Arms and in Scotland is called the Lord Lyon King of Arms. Coats of arms are normally issued for real people. Nowadays, lots of country and businesses also have coats of arms. Each symbol on the coat of arms will wikt:represent wikt:something that has an wikt:important wikt:meaning to that person, country or company. Coats of arms have many base shield designs that can be added on to, such as the Fess, Pale, Bend (heraldry), Chevron (insignia), Saltire, and many more. These examples shown are called "Ordinaries". An example of an item to add on to the base designs are called Charge (heraldry). These charges can be classified as emblems added on to the coat of arms base shield. It is also optional to have a supporter. In simple terms, the supporter is usually an animal seeming like it is holding up the shield of the coat of arms. The Royal Coat of Arms of The United Kingdom seen below is an example of supporters. Lastly, the final main piece of many coats of arms is called the Crest (heraldry). The crest is a symbol of some sorts usually resting on top of the shield base of the coat of arms.
