In 1818, the Lenape gave their territory to the U.S. in the Treaty of St. Mary's. p. 1042 In 1821, Indianapolis was made as a planned city to be the new capital of Indiana. The old capital was Corydon, Indiana. The city was Plat by Alexander Ralston and Elias Pym Fordham on a grid next to the White River (Indiana). The finishing of the National Road and Michigan Road Road and the start of railroad Construction made the city an important transport hub. Two of the nicknames for Indianapolis (the "Crossroads of America" and "Railroad City") matches the city's history with transportation. In 1970, Indianapolis and Marion County were City-county consolidation. This system is called Unigov. The city is led by an Election 25-member Indianapolis City-County Council led by the List of mayors of Indianapolis. Indianapolis has the List of U.S. metropolitan areas by GDP in the U.S. It is based on the Industry of trade, transportation, utilities, business Service sector, education, Welfare, government, leisure, and manufacturing. The city has big amateur sports and auto racing markets. The city is home to three Fortune 500 Company, two major league sports teams, the Indianapolis Colts of the National Football League and the Indiana Pacers of the National Basketball Association, five university Campus, and many Museum. Indianapolis has The Children's Museum of Indianapolis. The city is maybe best known for hosting the world's largest single-day sporting event, the Indianapolis 500. The city has many National Register of Historic Places listings in Marion County, Indiana.
