'''''Midnight Cowboy''''' is a 1969 United States drama movie, released by United Artists. John Schlesinger directed it, and Waldo Salt wrote the screenplay based on the James Leo Herlihy novel. It stars Dustin Hoffman (in his first starring role after ''The Graduate''), along with Jon Voight in the title role. It is the only MPAA movie to win an Academy Award for Best Picture. Schlesinger won a Best Director Award; both Hoffman and Voight were nominated for Best Actor. In 1971, it was given the "R" rating by the MPAA. Apart from this, nothing was changed in the movie. == Storyline == Joe Buck (played by Voight, in his first major acting role) was raised by his grandmother in Texas. She died after Joe grew up, when he was :wikt:drafted into the US Army. He had a girlfriend, who was called Crazy Annie, but she had been sent to a psychiatric hospital. With both women gone from his life, Joe had no-one in his life. When he first meets Rico "Ratso" Rizzo (Hoffman's character), Ratso (tricks) Joe out of United States dollar20, but when they met again, Ratso offered to share his "place", which turned out to be a room in a condemned building. The two became friends. Ratso shares what he knew about New York with Joe, and became his "manager" (pimp), and Joe shares any money he makes with Ratso. Ratso is sick, probably with tuberculosis, and as time goes on he depends more and more on Joe. Ratso plans to move to Florida before winter starts. The weather turns cold as the year ended, but Joe and Ratso got a break, when they were invited to a big party. Joe buys new clothes for them both, and throws away his cowboy clothing. "I ain't no kinda hustler," Joe decided, and he plans to find a regular job once they reached Florida. Joe and Ratso talk and joke during the trip, but Ratso dies before they arrived. Now he had lost his best friend, and Joe is scared to go on alone. ==Awards and honors== The movie won Academy Awards for Academy Award for Best Picture, Academy Award for Directing, and Academy Award for Writing Adapted Screenplay. Both Hoffman and Voight were nominated for Academy Award for Best Actor awards and Sylvia Miles was nominated for Academy Award for Best Supporting Actress. Miles had one of the shortest performances ever nominated (clocking in under four minutes of screen-time). The film won six British Academy Film Awards. It was also entered into the 19th Berlin International Film Festival. John Barry (composer), who supervised the music and composed the score for the movie, won a Grammy Award for Best Instrumental Composition. Fred Neil's song "Everybody's Talkin'" also won a Grammy Award for Best Male Pop Vocal Performance, for Harry Nilsson.
