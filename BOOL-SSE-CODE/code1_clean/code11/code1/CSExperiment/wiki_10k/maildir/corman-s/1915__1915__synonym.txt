Subject: Synonym
A '''synonym''' is a word with the same meaning as another word.Definition of synonym from Oxford Dictionaries Online. OxfordDictionaries.com, 2011. web: [http://oxforddictionaries.com/definition/synonym OD-syn] .Synonym - Definition and more from the Free Merriam-Webster Dictionary. 2011. webpage: [http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/synonym MW-syn]. ==Examples== * ''sick'' and ''Disease'' * ''child'' and ''kid'' * ''baby'' and ''infant'' * ''rich'' and ''Wealth'' * ''make'' and ''Creation'' * ''student'' and ''pupil'' * ''car'' and ''automobile'' * ''scared'' and ''frightened'' ==Nature== Synonyms can be Noun, Verb, Adverb or Adjective, but both words must be of the same part of speech. That means, only a noun can be a synonym of another noun, only a verb can be a synonym of another verb, and so on. One can find sets of synonyms in a thesaurus, which is a book listing groups of related Word. Some words are near-synonyms, which have similar but not exactly the same meaning. The companion to language assessment 4 (2013): 1790-1798. ==History== The word ''synonym'' dates back over 500 Year, to late Middle English. The word is Derivation from Latin from the Ancient Greek language word ''sunōnumon'', Neuter gender form of the adjective ''sunōnumos'', from ''sun-'' meaning 'with' + ''onoma'' meaning 'name' in the Greek language. The meaning of the word has remained unchanged for all these Century. There is even a saying, dating to 1892, ''Once a synonym, always a synonym''. ''Science'', John Michels, American Association for the Advancement of Science, 1892, page 220, web: [https://books.google.com/books?id=58U-AQAAIAAJ&pg=PA220 BG-AJ]. The word has been taught to Generation of English language Student and is commonly known by the public.
